Topic: Natural convection

Introduction to Natural Convection

  • Occurs due to density differences (caused by differences in temperature).
  • Key factors:
    • Temperature gradient
    • Fluid properties
    • Gravitational force

Applications of Natural Convection

  • Natural convection plays a crucial role in various applications:
    • Heating and Cooling Systems: Used in radiators, heat exchangers, and HVAC systems.
    • Environmental Science: Understanding weather patterns, ocean currents, and climate change.
    • Engineering Processes: Cooling of electronic devices, nuclear reactors, and industrial processes.
    • Architecture: Passive cooling and heating in buildings.
    • Biological Systems: Blood circulation and respiratory systems.

Comparison of Natural Convection with Other Heat Transfer Mechanisms

  • Conduction:
    • Heat transfer through a solid or stationary fluid.
    • Occurs due to temperature gradients.
    • Does not involve fluid movement.
  • Forced Convection:
    • Heat transfer through a moving fluid.
    • Involves external forces like fans or pumps.
    • Typically more efficient than natural convection.
  • Radiation:
    • Heat transfer through electromagnetic waves.
    • Does not require a medium (can occur in a vacuum).
    • Depends on surface properties and temperature.
  • Natural Convection:
    • Heat transfer through fluid movement due to density differences.
    • Occurs without external forces.
    • Less efficient than forced convection but important in many natural and engineering processes.

Thermal Expansion Coefficient in Natural Convection

  • The thermal expansion coefficient ($\beta$) is a measure of how much a fluid expands when heated.
  • It is defined as:
    • $$ \beta = \frac{1}{V} \left(\frac{\partial V}{\partial T}\right)_P = -\frac{1}{\rho} \left(\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial T}\right)_P $$
  • In natural convection, $\beta$ affects the buoyancy force:
    • Higher $\beta$ leads to stronger buoyancy forces.
    • Stronger buoyancy forces enhance fluid movement.
  • For ideal gases, $\beta$ is constant and given by:
    • $$ \beta_{ideal~gas} = \frac{1}{T}\hspace{30pt}(1/\text{K}) $$

Natural convection along vertical flat plate

  • Consider fluid rising in the vicinity of hot vertical wall
  • At the wall surface ($y=0$), we know:
    • Velocity is zero at the surface, e.g. $u(x,0)=0$ and $v(x,0)=0$.
    • Temperature is constant, e.g. $T(x,0)=T_s$.
  • Sufficiently far from the surface ($y\rightarrow \infty$), we know:
    • Velocity decreases towards zero, e.g. $u(x,\infty) \rightarrow 0$.
    • Temperature decreases towards surroundings temperature, e.g. $T(x,\infty) \rightarrow T_\infty$.
  • At the bottom ($x=0$), we know:
    • Velocity is zero, e.g. $u(0,y)=0$.
    • Temperature is at surroundings temperature, e.g. $T(0,y)=T_\infty$.

Equation of motion for natural convection

  • Now consider small volume element in the boundary layer
  • Newton's second law:
    • $$ \delta m \cdot a_x = F_x $$
  • Re-writing the terms:
    • Mass: $$ \delta m = \rho \delta x \delta y $$
    • Acceleration obtained by total diffential: $$ \text{d}u = \frac{\partial u}{\partial x} \text{d}x + \frac{\partial u}{\partial y} \text{d}y $$
      • Then acceleration becomes (by dividing by $\text{d}t$): $$ a_x = \frac{\text{d}u}{\text{d}t} = \frac{\partial u}{\partial x} \frac{\text{d}x}{\text{d}t} + \frac{\partial u}{\partial y} \frac{\text{d}y}{\text{d}t} = \frac{\partial u}{\partial x}u + \frac{\partial u}{\partial y}v $$
    • Force: $$ F_x = \left(\tau+\frac{\partial \tau}{\partial y} \text{d}y\right) - \tau + P - \left(P + \frac{\partial P}{\partial x} \text{d}x\right) - \rho g (\text{d}x \cdot \text{d}y) $$
  • Inserting in Newton's second law equation, dividing by $\delta x \delta y$ and inserting $\tau=\mu(\partial u/\partial y)$:
    • $$ \rho \left(\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}u + \frac{\partial u}{\partial y}v\right) = \mu \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial y^2} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial x} - \rho g $$

Equation of motion for natural convection

  • Far away from surface ($y\rightarrow \infty$), the velocity is zero and we obtain:$.
    • $$ \frac{\partial P_\infty}{\partial x} = -\rho_\infty g $$
  • Re-writing the terms:
    • Mass: $$ \delta m = \rho \delta x \delta y $$
    • Acceleration obtained by total diffential: $$ \text{d}u = \frac{\partial u}{\partial x} \text{d}x + \frac{\partial u}{\partial y} \text{d}y $$
      • Then acceleration becomes (by dividing by $\text{d}t$): $$ a_x = \frac{\text{d}u}{\text{d}t} = \frac{\partial u}{\partial x} \frac{\text{d}x}{\text{d}t} + \frac{\partial u}{\partial y} \frac{\text{d}y}{\text{d}t} = \frac{\partial u}{\partial x}u + \frac{\partial u}{\partial y}v $$
    • Force: $$ F_x = \left(\tau+\frac{\partial \tau}{\partial y} \text{d}y\right) - \tau + P - \left(P + \frac{\partial P}{\partial x} \text{d}x\right) - \rho g (\text{d}x \cdot \text{d}y) $$
  • Inserting the above in Newton's second law equation, dividing by $\delta x \delta y$ and inserting $\tau=\mu(\partial u/\partial y)$:
    • $$ \rho \left(\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}u + \frac{\partial u}{\partial y}v\right) = \mu \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial y^2} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial x} - \rho g $$

Transition to turbulence in natural convection

  • Natural convection can transition from laminar to turbulent flow.
  • Depends on the dimensionless Grashof number (Gr):
    • Defined as: $$ \text{Gr} = \frac{g \beta (T_s - T_\infty) y^3}{\nu^2} $$ where:
      • $g$ is the gravitational acceleration.
      • $\beta$ is the thermal expansion coefficient.
      • $(T_s - T_\infty)$ is the temperature difference between the surface and the surroundings.
      • $y$ is the vertical position on the plate (vertical position of plate).
      • $\nu$ is the kinematic viscosity.
  • For a vertical plate, the critical Grashof number is approx $10^9$.
